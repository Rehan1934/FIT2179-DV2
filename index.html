Malaysia’s Student Mental Health Pressure

How university survey data, youth warning signs, and global comparisons reveal a wider mental health concern.

Domain Student mental health and public-health awareness
Audience Average Malaysians, especially students, families and educators
Focus Reported symptoms, support gaps, youth warning signs and regional context

Why this matters

Mental health pressure among students is often hidden until it appears through anxiety, depression, panic attacks, loneliness, bullying or low support-seeking. This data story combines a Malaysian university student survey, adolescent health indicators and global prevalence estimates to show where warning signs appear and why support needs to be easier to discuss.

Important: The student survey is a small snapshot, not a national estimate. The visualisations show reported symptoms and warning signs, not clinical diagnosis or causation.

What the data suggests

Symptoms are visible. Depression, anxiety and panic attacks were all reported by a substantial share of student respondents.
Support is not equally visible. Specialist treatment-seeking is much lower than reported symptoms in the student survey.
Pressure starts early. Adolescent warning signs suggest that support should begin before university.
Context matters. Malaysia’s pattern is easier to understand when compared with nearby countries and longer-term estimates.

Data sources & methodology

Student survey

Student Mental Health dataset from Kaggle/IIUM. Used for depression, anxiety, panic attacks, CGPA, gender, year of study and treatment-seeking.

Global comparison

Mental Health Depression Disorder dataset. Used for Malaysia and ASEAN depression/anxiety trends and the geographic map.

Youth indicators

GHSH pooled adolescent dataset. Used for Malaysia adolescent warning signs such as attempted suicide, bullying and social support indicators.

Limitations

The student survey is small and not nationally representative. Global estimates are modelled. Charts show patterns and warning signs, not medical diagnosis or causation.
